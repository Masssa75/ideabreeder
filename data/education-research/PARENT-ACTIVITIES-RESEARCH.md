# Research: What Parents Should Do Instead of Homework

## Summary

Research consistently shows that **parental help with homework has no effect on elementary achievement** (Penn State), while other activities have strong positive effects on learning outcomes.

---

## 1. Parent Homework Help: No Effect

**Source:** [Penn State University Study](https://www.psu.edu/news/education/story/study-finds-parental-help-homework-has-no-impact-student-achievement)

> "Using sophisticated statistical models... researchers found no effect of parental help with homework on elementary students' math and reading achievement."

**Why it doesn't work:**
- Parents may lack education/developmental psychology background
- They often just give the correct answer (negates cognitive benefit)
- Can undermine children's sense of autonomy
- "Intrusive homework support" predicts decreased achievement (PMC study)

---

## 2. Parental Expectations: Strongest Predictor

**Source:** [Educational Psychology Review - Meta-analysis](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-010-9121-z)

> "Two meta-analyses have found that parental expectations are the strongest family-level predictor of student achievement outcomes, exceeding the variance accounted for by other parental beliefs and behaviors by a substantial margin."

**Effect size:** r = 0.30 (cross-sectional), r = 0.28 (longitudinal)

**How it works:**
- Transmission of positive expectations to the offspring
- Encouragement of academic engagement
- More effective than "behavioral academic involvement such as checking homework"

---

## 3. Family Dinner Conversation: 7x More Vocabulary

**Source:** [Harvard Graduate School of Education - Dr. Catherine Snow](https://www.gse.harvard.edu/ideas/edcast/20/04/benefit-family-mealtime)

> "Young kids learned 1,000 rare words at the dinner table, compared to only 143 from parents reading storybooks aloud."

**Additional findings:**
- Mealtime conversation has 10x more rare/uncommon words than other activities
- For school-age children, family dinners are "a more powerful predictor of high achievement scores than time spent in school, doing homework, playing sports or doing art"
- Children who eat 5-7 family dinners per week more likely to report mostly As and Bs
- Kindergarteners who can tell good stories grow into middle schoolers with better reading comprehension

---

## 4. Reading Aloud (Even to Older Kids)

**Source:** [PMC Meta-analysis of 29 studies](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3319370/)

> "Significant, positive effects on children's language, phonological awareness, print concepts, comprehension, and vocabulary outcomes were found."

**Key insights:**
- Picture books offer more diverse vocabulary than child-directed conversations
- Benefits don't stop when kids can read themselves
- Literacy expert Sue Pimentel: "I have no idea why teachers stop reading aloud to kids. It's a mistake."
- **Read texts 2-3 grades above their level** - they can comprehend more than they can decode

---

## 5. Unstructured Free Play: Builds Executive Function

**Source:** [Frontiers in Psychology / PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4060299/)

> "The more time children spent in less structured activities, the better their self-directed executive function, even when controlling for age, verbal ability, and household income."

**Critical finding:**
> "Conversely, structured activities predicted poorer self-directed EF."

**Why it matters:**
- Executive function predicts academic success and life outcomes
- Free play develops goal-setting, attention management, behavior regulation
- American Academy of Pediatrics recommends protecting unstructured play time

---

## 6. Outdoor Play / Nature

**Sources:**
- [PLOS One Systematic Review](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0229006)
- [Child Encyclopedia](https://www.child-encyclopedia.com/outdoor-play/according-experts/influence-outdoor-play-social-and-cognitive-development)

**Findings:**
- Preschoolers with 3+ hours outdoor play per day: advantages in early learning skills, self-regulation, social-emotional development
- 3-year longitudinal study: weekly forest school → improvements in reading, writing, math
- Nature play particularly impacts cognitive domains: imagination, creativity, dramatic play
- Anthony Pellegrini research: outdoor play breaks maximize attention to cognitive tasks

---

## 7. Sleep

**Sources:**
- [npj Science of Learning](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41539-019-0055-z)
- [University of Washington Study](https://www.washington.edu/news/2018/12/12/high-school-start-times-study/)

**Key finding:**
> "Sleep quality, duration, and consistency accounted for nearly 25% of the variance in academic performance."

**Seattle study:**
- Start times pushed later → 34 extra minutes of sleep
- Result: grades improved 4.5%

**Recommendations:**
- Ages 6-13: 9-11 hours per night
- Sleep consistency matters (same bedtime/wake time)
- 23% of children get only 8 hours; 8% get 7 or less

---

## Hierarchy of What Works (By Evidence Strength)

### Tier 1: Strongest Evidence
1. **Parental expectations** - r = 0.30, replicated across meta-analyses
2. **Family dinner conversation** - Harvard study, 7x vocabulary advantage
3. **Reading aloud** - 29-study meta-analysis

### Tier 2: Strong Evidence
4. **Unstructured play** - Executive function benefits, AAP recommended
5. **Sleep** - 25% of grade variance
6. **Outdoor play** - Multiple studies, forest school longitudinal data

### Tier 3: No Evidence / Negative
- **Homework help** - No effect (Penn State)
- **Structured activities** - Negative association with executive function
- **Homework itself (elementary)** - Zero to negative correlation
